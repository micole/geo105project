It all started in Idaho

A volcanic eruption caused ash to fly high into the air. Wind carrying the ash to Nebraska. As the ash settles on the food and water source for the native animals there, the small animals start dying from inhailing the tiny shards of glass. Over time the larger animals starve, and collapse.








Ecosystem

Across the Northern Hemisphere were wide expanses of grasslands that supported a variety of new mammals (Because of the warm climate, that started wet, and became dry grasslands).

  • Giant Perrisodactyl Morpus
  • Daphoenodon Dogs
  • Merychyus
  • Extinct horses, Parahippus
  • Semiaquatic Promerycochoerus
  • Large Pig-like Daeodons
  • Small camelid, Stenomylus









Map

During the Middle Miocene most of the landmass of North America is in place, with the exception of the east coast being underwater, and the north having more land mass than now (USA is connected to Greenland at this time, and the Hudson bay is above sea level).

During this time the continents are moving towards one another, coming inwards to each other. Since convergent boundaries have reverse faults, the force is compressing the plates together, creating the volcano that caused the ashfall beds.












Time Slice

Miocene is the seond longest epoch of the Cenozoic era (23.03 - 5.332 MYA).



Depositional Environment

The depositional environment of the Ashfall Beds in Nebraska during the Miocene Period contained floodplains that were forested, which bordered river channels. The environments were terrestrial, also containing lakes and rivers. The sediment that was deposited there during the Miocence period about fourteen million years ago was sand that was cross-bedded and pebbles of clay.

About twelve million years ago it changed a little to silt, and clayey sand. Before there was a volcanic eruption, the depositional environment was savannah-like grasslands. The ashfall skeletons are covered by a layer of volcanic ash. Igneous rock and sedimentary rock is now covering the area.



Rock Dating

The method used to date the rocks in the Ashfall Beds is absolute dating. It was first dated by counting the number of uranium scratches on the particles of glass shards. But more recently it has been dated by measuring the decay of argon isotopes to argon isotopes.
